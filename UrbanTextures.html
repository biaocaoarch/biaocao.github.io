Urban Textures:
A Campus in Shenzhen

My thesis is called Urban Textures: A Campus in Shenzhen. The project came from my long-term observation of how Chinese cities grow and how fast development often ignores the connection between buildings, landscape, and people. I wanted to study the deeper patterns that shape a city, not just the buildings themselves.

Shenzhen has developed extremely quickly, and the result is that many places lack real community spaces. Infrastructure is often separated from daily life, and large areas of the city are built in a way that doesn’t support slower, human activities. So in my thesis, I focused on the textures that make up the city—circulation, landscape, drainage, public spaces—and how these layers interact.

I designed the project as a campus because a campus is like a small city. It needs places for movement, gathering, quiet spaces, open areas, and different scales of activity. By studying patterns and layering them, I explored how infrastructure and public space could create a more balanced urban environment. The design looks simple at first, but it’s based on many observations of how people move, where they gather, and what kinds of spaces are missing in fast-growing cities.

For me, the project was not just about a single site. It was a way to think about the city as a system, how textures overlap, and how small changes in circulation or open space can influence the experience of a whole community. It reflects my interest in urban design and my curiosity about how architecture can respond to larger urban issues.
